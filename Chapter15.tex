\subsubsection{Stereochemical Requirements of the Diels–AlderTransition State}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{s}-cis Conformation of the Diene
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The diene must be in the \textit{s}-cis conformation to react.
        \item Dienes with functional groups that hinder the \textit{s}-cis conformation react more slowly than butadiene. Dienes with functional groups that hinder the \textit{s}-trans conformation react faster than butadiene.
    \end{enumerate}
    
    \item syn Stereochemistry
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item There is no opportunity for any of the substituents to change their stereochemical positions during the course of the reaction.
    \end{enumerate}
    
    \item The Endo Rule
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Dienophile has a pi bond in its electron-withdrawing group (as in a carbonyl group or a cyano group).
        
        \item Secondary overlap: an overlap of the \textit{p} orbitals of the electron-withdrawing group with the \textit{p} orbitals of C2 and C3 of the diene. 
        
        \item Secondary overlap helps to stabilize the transition state.
        
        \item This position is called the endo position, and This stereochemical preference for the electron-withdrawing substituent to appear in the endo position is called the endo rule.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Ultraviolet Light and Electronic Transitions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A photon with the right amount of energy can excite an electron from the bonding orbital ($ \pi $) to the antibonding orbital ($ \pi* $). This transition from a bonding orbital to a antibonding orbital is called a $ \pi \rightarrow \pi* $ transition.

    \item In conjugated systems, there are electronic transitions with lower energies that correspond to wavelengths longer than \qty{200}{\nano\meter}.

    \item In general, the energy difference between HOMO and LUMO decreases as the length of conjugation increases.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Obtaining an Ultraviolet Spectrum}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The sample solution is placed in a quartz cell, and some of the solvent is placed in a reference cell.
    \item Compare the amount of light transmitted through the sample (the sample beam) with the amount of light in the reference beam. Measure the intensity ratio of the reference ($ I_\text{r} $) beam compared with the sample beam ($ I_\text{s} $).
    \item The absorbance, $ A $, of the sample at a particular wavelength is governed by \textit{Beer’s law}.
    \begin{equation*}
        A = \log{\frac{I_\text{r}}{I_\text{s}}} = \epsilon c l
    \end{equation*}
    \item Molar absorptivity ($ \epsilon $) is a measure of how strongly the sample absorbs light at that wavelength.
    \item The molar absorptivity of onjugated system is about 5000 to 50000.  % unit?
\end{enumerate}
