\section{Reactions of Aromatic Compounds}

\subsection{Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution 20260326}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Although benzene’s pi electrons are in a stable aromatic system, they are available to attack a strong electrophile to give a carbocation.
    \item This resonance-stabilized carbocation is called a \textbf{sigma complex} because the electrophile is joined to the benzene ring by a new sigma bond.
    \item The sigma complex (also called an arenium ion) is not aromatic because the \textit{sp}$^3$ hybrid carbon atom interrupts the ring of \textit{p} orbitals.
    \begin{figure}[h]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{17.1_Sigma_Complex.png}
        \caption{Sigma Complex}
        \label{17.1_Sigma_Complex}
    \end{figure}
\end{enumerate}

\begin{mechanism}
    Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Step 1: Attack on the electrophile forms the sigma complex.
        \item Step 2: Loss of a proton regains aromaticity and gives the substitution product.
    \end{itemize}

    \begin{example} % no sequence
        Iodination of toluene
    \end{example}
\end{mechanism}

\subsubsection{Bromination of Benzene}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Bromination follows the general mechanism for electrophilic aromatic substitution.
    \item Bromine itself is not sufficiently electrophilic, and the formation of \ce{Br+} is difficult.
    \item A strong Lewis acid such as catalyzes the reaction by forming a complex with \ce{Br2} that reacts like \ce{Br+}.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{mechanism}
    Bromination of Benzene
\end{mechanism}

\subsubsection{Chlorination of Benzene}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Chlorination of benzene works much like bromination, except that aluminum chloride (\ce{AlCl3}) is most often used as the Lewis acid catalyst.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Iodination of Benzene}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Iodination of benzene requires an acidic oxidizing agent, such as nitric acid, to produce iodide cation.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{example}
    flourination
\end{example}

\subsection{Nitration of Benzene 20260326}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Benzene reacts with hot, concentrated nitric acid to give nitrobenzene. A safer and more convenient procedure uses a mixture of nitric acid and sulfuric acid.
    \item Sulfuric acid is a catalyst, allowing nitration to take place more rapidly and at lower temperatures.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{mechanism}
    Nitration of Benzene
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Preliminary steps: Formation of the nitronium ion, \ce{NO2+}.
        \item Step 1: Attack on the electrophile forms the sigma complex.
        \item Step 2: Loss of a proton gives nitrobenzene.
    \end{itemize}
\end{mechanism}

\begin{enumerate}[resume]
    \item Aromatic nitro groups are easily reduced to amino (\chemfig{-NH_2}) groups by treatment with an active metal such as tin, zinc, or iron in dilute acid.
    \item Nitration followed by reduction is often the best method for adding an amino group to an aromatic ring.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Sulfonation of Benzene 20260326}

\subsubsection{Sulfonation}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Sulfur trioxide (\ce{SO3}) is the electrophile.
    \item “Fuming sulfuric acid” is the common name for a solution of 7\% \ce{SO3} in \ce{H2SO4}.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{mechanism}
    Sulfonation of Benzene
\end{mechanism}

\subsubsection{Desulfonation}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A sulfonic acid group may be removed from an aromatic ring by heating in dilute sulfuric acid.
    \item Steam is often used as a source of both water and heat for desulfonation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Nitration of Toluene: The Effect of Alkyl Substitution 20260326}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Toluene (methylbenzene) reacts with a mixture of nitric and sulfuric acids much like benzene does, but with some interesting differences:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Toluene reacts about 25 times faster than benzene under the same conditions. Toluene is activated toward electrophilic aromatic substitution, and the methyl group is an \textbf{activating group}.
        \item Nitration of toluene gives a mixture of products, primarily those resulting from substitution at the ortho and para positions. The methyl group of toluene is an \textbf{ortho, para-director}.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item In ortho or para substitution of toluene, the positive charge is spread over two secondary carbons and one tertiary ($ 3^{\circ} $) carbon (bearing the \ce{CH3} group).
    \item The sigma complex for meta substitution has its positive charge spread over three $ 2^{\circ} $ carbons.
\end{enumerate}
