\section{Carboxylic Acids}

\subsection{Introduction 20260512}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The combination of a carbonyl group and a hydroxyl on the same carbon atom is called a \textbf{carboxyl group}. Compounds containing the carboxyl group are called \textbf{carboxylic acids}.
\end{enumerate}
\chemnameinit{}
$$\chemname{\chemfig{-C(=[2]O)-O-H}}{carboxyl group}$$
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
    \item An \textbf{aliphatic acid} has an alkyl group bonded to the carboxyl group, and \textbf{an aromatic} acid has an aryl group. \textbf{Fatty acids} are long-chain aliphatic acids derived from the hydrolysis of fats and oils.
    \item A carboxylic acid donates protons by heterolytic cleavage of the acidic \chemfig{O-H} bond to give a proton and a \textbf{carboxylate ion}.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Structure and Physical Properties of Carboxylic Acids 20260512}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Structure of the Carboxyl Group
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The \textit{sp}$^2$ hybrid carbonyl carbon atom is planar, with nearly trigonal bond angles. The \chemfig{O-H} bond also lies in this plane, eclipsed with the \chemfig{C=O} bond.
        \item One of the unshared electron pairs on the hydroxyl oxygen atom is delocalized into the electrophilic pi system of the carbonyl group.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Boiling Points
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Carboxylic acids boil at considerably higher temperatures than do alcohols, ketones, or aldehydes of similar molecular weights.
        \item The high boiling points of carboxylic acids result from formation of a stable, hydrogenbonded dimer.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Melting Points
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Aliphatic acids with more than 8 carbons are solids at room temperature.
        \item The presence of double bounds lowers the melting points. 
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Solubilities
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Form hydrogen bond with water.
        \item Acids with up to 4 carbons are miscible in water.
        \item Very soluble in alcohols.
        \item Most are quite soluble in relatively nonpolar solvents.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Acidity of Carboxylic Acids 20260512}

\subsubsection{Measurement of Acidity}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A carboxylic acid may dissociate in water to give a proton and a carboxylate ion. The equilibrium constant $K_{\mathrm{a}}$ for this reaction is called the \textit{acid-dissociation constant}.
    \item Values of $\mathrm{p}K_\mathrm{a}$ are about 5 for simple carboxylic acids. Benzoic acid $\mathrm{p}K_\mathrm{a} = 4.19$, acetic acid $\mathrm{p}K_\mathrm{a} = 4.7$, phenol $\mathrm{p}K_\mathrm{a} \sim 10$.
    \item The acid will be mostly dissociated if the $\mathrm{pH}$ of the solution is higher than the $\mathrm{p}K_\mathrm{a}$ of the acid.
    \item The carboxylate ion can be visualized either as a resonance hybrid  or as a conjugated system. There is half of a $\pi$ bond between the carbon and each oxygen atom, and half of a negative charge on each oxygen atom.
    \item The second carboxyl group is always less acidic than the first in dicarboxylic acid.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Substituent Effects on Acidity}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Substituent that stabilizes the negatively charged carboxylate ion promotes dissociation and results in a stronger acid.
    \item The magnitude of a substituent effect depends on its distance from the carboxyl group.
    \item Substituted benzoic acids show similar trends in acidity, with electron-withdrawing groups enhancing the acid strength and electron-donating groups decreasing the acid strength.
    \item Effects are strongest for substituents in the ortho and para positions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Salts of Carboxylic Acids 20260512}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A strong base can completely deprotonate a carboxylic acid. The combination of a carboxylate ion and a cation is a \textbf{salt of a carboxylic acid}.
    \item Addition of a mineral acid converts a carboxylic acid salt back to the original carboxylic acid.
\end{enumerate}

\ch{<=>>} \quad
\chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-O^{-}} \ch{M^+}}{acid salt} \quad
+ \quad \chemname{\ch{H2O}}{water}$$
$$\chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-O^{-}} \ch{M^+}}{acid salt} \quad
+ \quad
\ch{H^+} \quad
\ch{<=>>} \quad
\chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-OH}}{regenerated acid} \quad
+ \quad \ch{M^+}$$
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
    \item Nomenclature of Carboxylic Acid Salts
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Salts of carboxylic acids are named by naming the cation, then naming the carboxylate ion by replacing the \textit{-ic acid} part of the acid name with \textit{-ate}.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Properties of Acid Salts
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Carboxylic acid salts are solids with little odor.
        \item Salts of the alkali metals and ammonium carboxylates are generally soluble in water but relatively insoluble in nonpolar organic solvents.
        \item Soap is the soluble sodium salt of a long chain fatty acid.
        \item Salt formation can be used to identify and purify acids by the reaction of an acid with \ch{NaHCO3}.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Purification methods
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item A carboxylic acid is more soluble in the organic phase, but its salt is more soluble in the aqueous phase.
        \item Acid–base extractions can move the acid from the ether phase into the aqueous phase and back into the ether phase, leaving impurities behind.

\subsection{Spectroscopy of Carboxylic Acids 20260514}

\subsubsection{Infrared Spectroscopy}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The intense carbonyl stretching vibration absorbs around (\qty{1710}{\per\centi\meter}). Conjugation lowers the frequency.
    \item The \chemfig{O-H} stretching vibration absorbs around (\qtyrange{2500}{3500}{\per\centi\meter}), lower than the hydroxyl stretching frequencies of water and alcohols. A broad peak at \qty{3000}{\per\centi\meter}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{NMR Spectroscopy}

\begin{enumerate}

\subsection{Condensation of Acids with Alcohols: The Fischer Esterification 20260514 20260519}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The \textbf{Fischer esterification} converts carboxylic acids and alcohols directly to esters by an acid-catalyzed nucleophilic acyl substitutison.
\end{enumerate}
\chemnameinit{}
$$\chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-OH}}{acid} \quad
+ \quad
\chemname{\chemfig{R'-OH}}{alcohol} \quad
\ch{<=>[ H^+ ]} \quad
\chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-O-R'}}{ester} \quad
+ \quad \ch{H2O} \quad$$

\begin{mechanism}
    Fischer Esterification
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{\textit{Part 1:}} Acid-catalyzed addition of the alcohol to the carbonyl group.
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Protonation activates the carbonyl.
            \item The alcohol adds.
            \item Deprotonation completes the reaction.
        \end{itemize}
        \begin{figure}[H]
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{20.10_Fischer_Esterification_1.png}
            \label{20.10_Fischer_Esterification_1}
        \end{figure}
        \item \textbf{\textit{Part 2:}} Acid-catalyzed dehydration.
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Protonation prepares the \ch{OH} group to leave.
            \item Water leaves.
            \item Deprotonation completes the reaction.
        \end{itemize}
        \begin{figure}[H]
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{20.10_Fischer_Esterification_2.png}
            \label{20.10_Fischer_Esterification_2}
        \end{figure}
    \end{itemize}
\end{mechanism}

\begin{enumerate}[resume]
    \item Reaction is an equilibrium, the yield of ester is not high.
    \item Esterification may be driven to the right either by using an excess of one of the reactants or by removing one of the products.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Reduction of Carboxylic Acids 20260519}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Lithium aluminum hydride (\ch{LiAlH4} or LAH) reduces carboxylic acids to primary alcohols.
    \item The intermediate aldehyde reacts faster than the carboxylic acid with the reducing agent. 
    \item Possible mechanism
    \begin{enumerate} % pic
        \item Lithium aluminum hydride is a strong base, and the first step is deprotonation of the acid. Hydrogen gas is evolved, and the lithium salt results.
        \item Several paths are possible for the rest of the mechanism. In one likely path, adds to the carbonyl group of the lithium carboxylate salt.
        \item Elimination gives an aldehyde, which is quickly reduced to a lithium alkoxide.
        \item Water added in the second step protonates the alkoxide to the primary alcohol.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Borane (\ch{BH3 $\cdot$ THF} or \ch{B2H6}) also reduces carboxylic acids to primary alcohols. 
    \item React with the carboxyl group faster than with any other carbonyl function, elcellent selectivity.
    \item Reduction to Aldehydes
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Lithium tri-tert-butoxyaluminum hydride, \ch{LiAlH(O-$t$-Bu)3} is a weaker reducing agent than lithium aluminum hydride. 
        \item Reduction of an acid to an aldehyde is a two-step process: Convert the acid to the acid chloride, then reduce using lithium tri-tert-butoxyaluminum hydride.
        \begin{figure}[H]
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{20.13_Reduction_to_Aldehydes.png}
            \label{20.13_Reduction_to_Aldehydes}
        \end{figure}
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Carboxylic acids react with two equivalents of an organolithium reagent to give ketones.
    \item Grignard reagents cannot be used.
    \item Mechanism: The first equivalent of the organolithium reagent simply deprotonates the acid. The second equivalent adds to the carbonyl to give a stable dianion. Hydrolysis of the dianion (by adding water) gives the hydrate of a ketone.

\end{enumerate}
