\section{Carboxylic Acid Derivatives}

\subsection{Introduction 20260519}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Carboxylic acid derivatives are compounds with functional groups that can be converted to carboxylic acids by a simple acidic or basic hydrolysis.
    \item The most important acid derivatives are esters, amides, and nitriles. Acid halides and anhydrides are also included.
\end{enumerate}
\chemnameinit{}
\begin{center}
    \chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-X}}{acid halide} \quad 
    \chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-O-C(=[2]O)-R}}{anhydride} \quad
    \chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-O-R'}}{ester} \quad 
    \chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-NH_2}}{amide} \quad 
    \chemname{\chemfig{R-C~N}}{nitrile} 
\end{center}
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
    \item Esters and amides are commonly found in nature.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Esters of Carboxylic Acids}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Esters} are carboxylic acid derivatives in which the hydroxyl group (\raisebox{0.75ex}{\chemfig{-OH}}) is replaced by an alkoxy group (\raisebox{0.75ex}{\chemfig{-OR}}).
    \item Esters can be formed by the Fischer esterification of an acid with an alcohol, excess alcohol is used.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Nitriles}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Nitriles} contain the \textbf{cyano group}, \raisebox{0.75ex}{\chemfig{-C~N}}.
    \item Nitriles hydrolyze to give carboxylic acids and can be synthesized by dehydration of amides.
\end{enumerate}
\chemnameinit{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-NH_2}}
$$\chemname{\chemfig{R-C~N}}{nitrile} \quad
\ch{->[ H2O][H^+\ or\ OH^-]} \quad
\chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-NH_2}}{primary amine} \quad
\ch{->[ H2O][H^+]} \quad
\chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-OH}}{acid}$$
$$\chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-OH}}{acid} \quad
\ch{->[ NH3][heat]} \quad
\chemname{\chemfig{R-C(=[2]O)-NH_2}}{primary amine} \quad
\ch{->[ POCl3]} \quad
\chemname{\chemfig{R-C~N}}{nitrile}$$
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
    \item Both \ch{C} and \ch{N} of the cyano group are \textit{sp} hybridized, and the \chemfig{R-C~N} bond angle is \qty{180}{\degree}. The structure is similar to that of a terminal alkyne, except that the nitrogen atom has a lone pair of electrons (tightly bound, not basic).
    \item Nomenclature
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Common name: Replace the suffix \textit{-ic acid} of the carboxylic acid with the suffix \textit{-onitrile}.
        \item IUPAC name: Add the suffix \textit{-nitrile} to the alkane name.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Acid Halides}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Acid halides} are also called \textbf{acyl halides}, used to synthesize other acid derivatives.
    \item The most common acyl halides: acid chlorides (acyl chlorides).
    \item Halogen atom inductively withdraws electron density from the carbonyl carbon, enhancing its electrophilic nature.
    \item Nomenclature
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Replace the \textit{-ic acid} suffix of the acid name with \textit{-yl} and the halide name (either the common name or the IUPAC name).
        \item For acids that are named as alkanecarboxylic acids, the acid chlorides are named by using the suffix \textit{-carbonyl chloride}.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Physical Properties of Carboxylic Acid  Derivatives 20260521}

\subsubsection{Boiling Points and Melting Points}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Esters and acid chlorides: near those of the unbranched alkanes. Small effect of the carbonyl group polarity.
    \item Carboxylic acids: elevated boiling points. Strongly hydrogen bonded in the liquid phase.
    \item Nitriles: higher boiling points than esters and acid chlorides. Strong dipolar association between adjacent cyano groups.
    \item Amides: surprisingly high boiling points and melting points. Melting points increase with increasing number of \chemfig{N-H} bonds.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Solubility}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Acid chlorides and anhydrides are too reactive to be used in nucleophilic solvents such as water and alcohols.
    \item Smaller esters, amides, and nitriles are relatively soluble in water because of their high polarity and their ability to form hydrogen bonds with water.
    \item Esters, tertiary amides, and nitriles are frequently used as solvents for organic reactions because they provide a polar reaction medium without \chemfig{O-H} or \chemfig{N-H} groups.
    \item Polar solvents commonly used: ethyl acetate, cetonitrile, dimethylformamide (DMF), and dimethylacetamide (DMA).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Interconversion of Acid Derivatives by Nucleophilic Acyl Substitution 20260521 20260526}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Most of the reactions of acid derivatives involve \textbf{nucleophilic acyl substitutions}, under both basic and acidic conditions.
    \item Nucleophilic acyl substitutions are also called \textbf{acyl transfer} reactions.
    \item Favorable reactions generally convert a more reactive acid derivative to a less reactive one.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Reactivity of Acid Derivative}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Acid derivatives differ greatly in their reactivity toward nucleophilic acyl substitution.
    \item Strong bases are not good leaving groups, and the reactivity of the derivatives decreases as the leaving group becomes more basic. %pic !!!
    \begin{figure}[H]
       \centering
       \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{21.5_Acid_Derivatives_Reactivity.png}
       %\caption{***}
       \label{21.5_Acid_Derivatives_Reactivity}
    \end{figure}
    \item Resonance stabilization also affects the reactivity of acid derivatives.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{note}
    Nearly all the reactions in this chapter are nucleophilic acyl substitutions that follow the addition–elimination mechanism under acidic or basic conditions.

    In the basic pathway, the nucleophile attacks the carbonyl oxygen to form a tetrahedral intermediate.Then the intermediate expels the leaving group to regenerate the carbonyl group. 
    
    In the acidic pathway, the acid catalyst protonates the carbonyl group so that the weak nucleophile can add. In most cases, the leaving group also becomes protonated before it leaves, so that it can leave as a weak base rather than as a strong base.
\end{note}

\subsubsection{Leaving Groups in Nucleophilic Acyl Substitutions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Strong bases are poor leaving groups for alkyl substitution and elimination reactions ($\mathrm{S_N}1$, $\mathrm{S_N}2$, E1, E2), but they may serve as leaving groups in acyl substitution.
    \item The $\mathrm{S_N}2$ reaction’s one-step mechanism is not strongly endothermic or exothermic.
    \item In the acyl substitution, the leaving group leaves in a separate second step. This second step is highly exothermic. 
\end{enumerate}

\begin{note}
    A strong base may serve as a leaving group if it leaves in a highly exothermic step, usually converting an unstable, negatively charged intermediate to a stable molecule.
\end{note}
